Legacy Band Members

Kino’s core lineup evolved over the years, but the group is most closely associated with its classic late-1980s formation, which defined their sound and legacy. Alongside frontman Viktor Tsoi (vocals, rhythm guitar), the band included Yuri Kasparyan (lead guitar), Georgy “Gustav” Guryanov (drums), and Igor Tikhomirov (bass). Earlier periods also featured musicians such as Aleksei Rybin (guitar) and Maksim Pashkov (bass), who contributed to the band’s formative years..

Victor Tsoi

Viktor Robertovich Tsoi (1962–1990) was a Soviet singer, songwriter, and the charismatic frontman of the rock band Kino, one of the most influential groups in the history of Russian-language music. Born in Leningrad, Tsoi showed artistic inclinations from a young age, studying at art schools and later immersing himself in the growing underground rock community of the early 1980s. His musical direction took shape when he met musicians such as Aleksei Rybin and Boris Grebenshchikov, eventually leading to the creation of Kino and the emergence of Tsoi as its principal creative force.

Tsoi became known for his minimalistic yet emotionally charged songwriting, blending post-punk influences with poetic, socially resonant lyrics that captured the mood, frustrations, and aspirations of late-Soviet youth. By the mid-to-late 1980s, Kino had risen to prominence through albums such as Gruppa Krovi (Blood Type) and Zvezda po Imeni Solzntse (A Star Called Sun), which solidified Tsoi as a cultural icon beyond the underground scene with his Anti War themes and struggle people endure within the system.

Outside of music, Tsoi was also active in film, appearing in several Soviet movies, most notably Assa (1987) and The Needle (1988), which helped expand his public presence. Despite his growing fame, he remained dedicated to his craft and lived a relatively modest life focused on writing and performing.

Viktor Tsoi’s life was tragically cut short in a car accident in 1990, but his impact has only grown in the decades since. His songs became symbols of change, resilience, and personal expression, and he is remembered today not only as a musician, but as a voice of a generation. His legacy continues through Kino’s enduring popularity, ongoing tribute performances, and the lasting cultural imprint of his work across the former Soviet Union and beyond. He was so loved by his fans to the point where some of his fans even killed themselves over his death.

Yuri Kasparyan

Yuri Dmitrievich Kasparyan (born 1963) is a Soviet and Russian musician best known as the lead guitarist of Kino and one of the defining creative forces behind the band’s sound. Born in Leningrad, he initially studied classical music and cello before transitioning to guitar in his teenage years, eventually becoming active in the city’s underground rock scene. His meeting with Viktor Tsoi in the early 1980s marked the beginning of a long and influential musical partnership.

Kasparyan joined Kino during the band’s formative period and quickly became central to its musical identity. His distinctive guitar style—minimalistic, melodic, atmospheric, and strongly influenced by post-punk—played a crucial role in shaping Kino’s sound on key albums such as Noch, Gruppa Krovi, and Zvezda po Imeni Solntse. His approach combined expressive riffs with sparse, carefully layered arrangements, giving Kino its unmistakable tonal character.

Following Tsoi’s passing in 1990, Kasparyan stepped away from public performance for several years but later returned to music, participating in various collaborative projects and studio work. In later decades, he became involved in tribute performances and commemorative concerts that honored Kino's legacy, often appearing as the central original member in modern interpretations of the band’s music.

Today, Yuri Kasparyan is widely regarded as one of the most influential guitarists in Russian rock history, remembered for his disciplined musicianship, innovative tone, and lasting contributions to the cultural impact of Kino.

Georgy “Gustav” Guryanov

Georgy Konstantinovich Guryanov (1961–2013), often known by the nickname “Gustav”, was a Russian musician, artist, and cultural figure best known as the drummer of the band Kino during its most influential period. He joined the group in 1984, becoming a key member of the classic lineup alongside Viktor Tsoi, Yuri Kasparyan, and Igor Tikhomirov.

Guryanov’s drumming style was distinctive for its precision, and restrained power, aligning closely with Kino’s post-punk aesthetic. Rather than complex or expressive fills, his playing emphasized steady rhythms, drum-machine–inspired patterns, and a mechanical consistency that helped define the band’s cold, atmospheric sound. This approach became especially prominent on albums such as Noch, Gruppa Krovi, and Zvezda po imeni Solntse, where the drums served as a driving but understated foundation for Tsoi’s vocals and Kasparyan’s guitar work.

Outside of Kino, Guryanov pursued a career in the visual arts, becoming an established painter associated with the New Academy of Fine Arts movement in Saint Petersburg. His artistic work reflected a fascination with classical aesthetics, Soviet symbolism, and idealized imagery, distinguishing him from many of his contemporaries in the rock scene.

After Viktor Tsoi’s death in 1990 and the subsequent dissolution of Kino, Guryanov withdrew from active participation in popular music, focusing primarily on his artistic career. Despite this, his contribution to Kino remains essential: his disciplined, minimalist drumming played a crucial role in shaping the band’s sound during its peak years.

Georgy “Gustav” Guryanov passed away in 2013, but he is remembered as an integral part of Kino’s legacy — a musician whose understated style and visual identity helped define one of the most important bands in Russian rock history.

Alexander Titov

Alexander Alexandrovich Titov (born 1957) is a Russian bass guitarist known for his involvement in Kino during the band’s early-to-mid 1980s period. He joined the group around 1984, at a time when Kino was transitioning from an experimental underground project into a more structured band within the Leningrad rock scene.

As Kino’s bassist, Titov contributed to the group’s developing sound during an important formative phase. His playing can be heard on recordings from the mid-1980s, including material associated with albums such as Nachalnik Kamchatki and Eto ne lyubov... During this period, Kino was refining its musical identity, moving toward the minimalist post-punk style that would later define its most well-known work.

Titov was already an established figure in the Leningrad music community and was also a member of the band Aquarium, which influenced his time with Kino. His experience helped support the group as they gained visibility through performances at the Leningrad Rock Club and participation in major rock festivals.

He left Kino in 1985, after which the bass role was later filled by Igor Tikhomirov. Despite his relatively brief tenure, Titov’s involvement played a role in Kino’s development during a critical stage of their rise.

In 2019, Alexander Titov returned to Kino as part of the band’s official reunion project, performing alongside Yuri Kasparyan and Igor Tikhomirov in concerts that honored Viktor Tsoi’s legacy.

Igor Tikhomirov

Igor Vladimirovich Tikhomirov (born 1959) is a Russian musician best known as the bass guitarist of Kino during the band’s most successful and influential period in the late 1980s. He joined Kino in 1986, replacing Alexander Titov, and became part of the band’s classic lineup alongside Viktor Tsoi, Yuri Kasparyan, and Georgy “Gustav” Guryanov.

He participated in the recording of several key releases, including Gruppa Krovi, Zvezda po imeni Solntse, and the posthumously released Chyorny Albom (commonly known as The Black Album). These albums marked the height of Kino’s popularity and established the group as a cultural phenomenon across the Soviet Union and beyond.

Following Viktor Tsoi’s death in 1990 and the subsequent disbandment of Kino, Tikhomirov continued working in music, though largely outside the mainstream spotlight. He collaborated with other artists from the Leningrad rock scene and remained connected to projects related to Kino’s legacy.

In later years, Tikhomirov took part in modern Kino reunion projects, performing alongside Yuri Kasparyan and other former members in tribute concerts and officially sanctioned revival tours. His participation helped preserve the authenticity of Kino’s sound for new audiences while honoring the band’s original spirit.
